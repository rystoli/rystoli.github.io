A dynamic overgeneralization model of face trait impressions¶

Here we simulate a model of trait impressions from a face. In this model, trait concepts (nodes) are associated with one another to varying degrees (paths). In other words, everyone has prior associations between different traits ('how often is a smart person kind?'). A face (represented by a set of features) activates certain traits (which prefer those features), from which spreading activation branches out to other traits. That is, in this model a face does not necessarily cause spontaneous trait inferences of all traits, rather, from a set of few spontaneous face trait inferences, other traits are inferred through associative processes. For instance, we see a face, infer that the person is kind, then come to inferences of other traits, such as creativity, based on those traits' associations with kindness. This is a temporal extension of the overgeneralization model (Zebrowitz & Montepare, 2008 - SPPC) of trait impressions from faces, where apparent inferences from a face elicit higher level personality inferences (eg, someone who looks subtly happy is inferred to be kind). However we extend those overgeneralizations farther in a hierarchical manner, where initial overgeneralizations lead to even more trait inferences associated with them (a nice person is often trustworthy). This model implies several important extensions to face trait models. First, we can make nuanced predictions about the temporal dynamics at which different trait inferences are made, and predict many traits inferred from a face with a small amount of information. Second, that dimensions may merely be a structural consequence of a limited set of face-feature-to-trait associations (Oosterhoff & Todorov, 2008 - PNAS). If only few independent trait nodes can be activated directly from a face, spreading activation may limited the entire trait space to that lower dimensional space. Also, there will be much higher reliability of in judgments directly made from faces. Whereas higher level judgments are derived from cascading associative processes in a connectionist network that varies between individuals largely.

  • Zebrowitz, L. A., & Montepare, J. M. (2008). Social psychological face perception: Why appearance matters. Social and Personality Psychology Compass, 2, 1497-1517.
  • Oosterhof, N. N., & Todorov, A. (2008). The functional basis of face evaluation. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 105(32), 11087-11092.

Specifically, this simulation at this point demonstrate the latter point made above. That there is reliability in trait judgments immediately made from a face, whereas traits then activated by associative processes will have greater variance due to network dynamics and differences between perceivers. Current perspectives largely posit we infer each of many traits directly from a faces visual traits (babyishness, subtle emotion, health, symmetry, gender). This perspective would therefore predict each trait be inferred with similar reliability. However, if many traits are activated merely through there association with initially inferred traits, they will vary in their activation between perceivers merely due to variance in face trait space, or each participants individual associations between traits. Eg, a subtly happy face may activate kindness in most perceivers, however the association between kindness and trustworthiness may vary between perceivers causing less reliabilty in the activation of trustworthiness. This could perhaps account for recent findings in systematic differences in the variance of different trait judgments from faces (Hehman et al., 2017 - JPSP).

  • Hehman, E., Sutherland, C. A. M., Flake, J. K., & Slepian, M. L. (in press). The unique contributions of perceiver and target characteristics in person perception. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology.

If the proposed model is accurate, the variance in trait judgments is related to each traits centrality in the associative network to the initially activated node. Here we test that prediction. We simulate spreading activation in an associative trait network per perceiver, starting at the same node (assuming they all see a face similarly in regards to subtle happiness). We then measure variance in activation of other trait nodes, and show it relates to the centrality of that node to the trait association network.

# synthesize trait associations
m = np.array([ np.zeros(n_subjects) for i in squareform(props) ])

    if int(np.round(squareform(props)[i]*n_subjects)) == 0: pass
    else: m[i][np.sort(random.sample(range(n_subjects),int(np.round(squareform(props)[i]*n_subjects))))] = 1

A_n = np.array([ squareform(m[:,s]) for s in range(n_subjects) ])

Simulation¶

Now we have proper data to simulate this process.

In this simulation, we allow a face stimulus to activate the central node, from which activation spreads continuously through the graph, activated each trait concept more or less. Per perceiver, we acquire final activation of each trait concept (node) (yes or no), from which we can calculate the between perceiver variance in judgment of each trait.

Now we estimate the spreading activation of trait nodes, as paths activated subsequent to each step of depth into the network.

WARNING: Code from hell¶

Here we estimate, after an initial node is activated, how the activation spreads out to other nodes. This is estimated per subject, giving us the probability each trait node activates at each depth of spreading activation. Less central nodes to the cluster should be more likely to activate at further depths.

In [6]:
# for each subject, we simulate depths of each trait node activation per face
# simulate trial, where face is compared to nodes, and activates one.
n_paths_per_depth = []
for s in range(n_subjects):
    paths_per_depth,depth = [[1]],0
    while np.sum(paths_per_depth[-1]) > 0:
        paths_per_depth.append( [int(i) for i in compute_paths(A_n[s],depth)[:,start_node]] ) 
        depth += 1
    n_paths_per_depth.append(np.array(paths_per_depth[1:]))

#correct issue of depth differences between different trials/subjects
maxdepth_n = [ ppds.shape[0] for ppds in n_paths_per_depth ]
maxdepth_m = np.max(maxdepth_n)
for i,ppd in enumerate(n_paths_per_depth):
    if ppd.shape[0] < maxdepth_m:
        for lessdepth in range(maxdepth_m - ppd.shape[0]):
            n_paths_per_depth[i] = np.vstack([n_paths_per_depth[i],np.zeros_like(ppd[0])])

mean_ppds_n = np.mean(n_paths_per_depth,axis=0)

Below, we plot the likelihood (across perceivers) of each trait node activating over time throughout the network. You will see each starts at the same trait node (e.g., subtle happiness), from which activation spreads out activating subsequent nodes, though less likely. Depth is the temporal order of the spreading activation, with 0 meaning the first node activated by the face stimulus, 1 being those nodes activated one step away, 2 another step, etc.
